ANOVA/REGWQ Homework©

    Read Correlated T-Test Homework for a brief description of the research which inspired me to write the simulator from which the data for this assignment came. Buss et al. also measured participants' pulse rates during the imagining. It is the pulse rate difference scores that were simulated for this assignment.  They were calculated as (pulse rate while imagining infidelity) minus (pulse rate at baseline).

    I have provided you with simulated data for each of the four groups: Men imagining sexual infidelity, men imagining emotional infidelity, women imagining sexual infidelity, and women imagining emotional infidelity. These data are deposited in my SimData directory on the core server and linked below.  Your file will have a name of the form 'Buss-nn,' where 'nn' is the identification number I use to identify your data files.  When you look at the data file you will find that each participant has one line of data. The first score for each participant is the code for the variable group, where 1 codes male, sexual image; 2 codes male, emotional image; 3 codes female, sexual image; and 4 codes female, emotional image. The second variable is gender/sex, where 1 codes male and 2 codes female. The third variable is type of imagined infidelity, where 1 codes sexual and 2 codes emotional. The final variable is pulse_increase (when interpreting your results, remember that this is a difference score, representing the amount by which pulse rate increased relative to the baseline neutral imagining -- a negative value would indicate that the pulse was lower after imagining infidelity).  The delimiter is a blank space -- there is a blank space between each score and the next.  There is one line of data for each subject.  You will not be using the gender/sex and type of imagined infidelity variables in this assignment, but will in a future assignment using the same data set.

    Please do use Proc Format and a Format statement to label the numeric values for the grouping variable (or SPSS Value Labels).

  • Use G*Power to answer the following questions:
    •  How many subjects would you need to have 80% power if the population effect size were small (f = 0.10) and you used a .05 criterion of statistical significance?
    •  If you were only able to obtain 96 subjects in each of the four groups, what would power be if the population effect size were small (f = 0.10)?
    •  If you were only able to obtain 96 subjects in each of the four groups, what would power be if the population effect size were medium (f = 0.25)?
  • Use a one-way independent samples ANOVA to test the null hypothesis that the four populations have identical means. Use a .05 criterion for significance.
  • Compute (semipartial) eta-squared and place a 90% confidence interval about it.  Why 90%?
  • Conduct pairwise comparisons using the REGWQ procedure. Hold familywise alpha at a maximum of .05.
  • Prepare a Word table which presents the results of the REGWQ procedure.  Model it after that shown in my document Presentation of a One-Way ANOVA.  This same document illustrates how to presents results in APA-style.
  • Send an email to all members of the class in which you briefly present the results of the REGWQ.  For example, "Pulse rate increases accompanying thoughts of sexual or emotional infidelity were significantly higher in men than in women.  All other differences fell short of statistical significance.  The subject line of that email should be "PSYC 7431:  Terse report of my REGWQ results."
    • Each of you has received a random sample from the same population from which all other students' data were sampled.  Accordingly, the class is conducting a mini Monte-Carlo simulation.
    • Do notice that the pattern of results differs somewhat across students/samples.
    • Keep this in mind when you try to replicate previously published research and you obtain results somewhat different than those obtained previously.
    • Such differences may well be due solely to sampling error, and you would be wasting your time trying to explain them in terms of possible differences with respect to subject population, procedural details, and so on.
  • Prepare a Word document (name the Word document "Nnnnn_REGWQ" where "Nnnnn" is your last name) which contains:
    • At the top, your answers to the questions about power,
    • Followed by an APA-style summary statement in which you present
      • Clear indication of what the groups are (they differ on both gender of subjects and type of imagined infidelity) and the variable on which the groups are being compared (the variable is not pulse rates, it is difference in pulse rates -- treatment minus baseline difference scores).
      • The results of the ANOVA (F, df, MSE, p, and both a point estimate of and a 90% confidence interval for eta-squared) -- do not present the pairwise comparisons before presenting the result of the omnibus ANOVA.
      • A table of means and standard deviations which indicates which means differ significantly from which other means.
      • A terse verbal description of the pattern of significant differences between means.
      • A brief interpretation of the results.
  • In a separate document, all of the statistical output.  Name this document "Nnnnn_Output" where "Nnnnn" is your last name
  • Attach both documents to a piece of email sent to me with the subject line "PSYC 7431:  ANOVA_REGWQ Homework."

    As you know, the REGWQ, and most other techniques for pairwise comparisons, were developed as a substitute for the ANOVA, not as a procedure to run after the ANOVA.  That said, editors and reviewers (ignorant folk) generally resist publishing a REGWQ analysis without an accompanying ANOVA.  Only once have I managed to get the REGWQ published without the ANOVA.  See Table 3 and the accompanying text in:

  • Aziz, S., Wuensch, K. L., & Brandon, H. R.  (2010).  A comparison among worker types using a composites approach and median splits.  The Psychological Record, 60, 627-642.

Tips.

  • Our grouping variable is comprised of three orthogonal (given equal sample sizes) effects: 1. The effect of sex/gender ignoring type of imagined infidelity, 2. The effect of type of imagined infidelity ignoring sex/gender, and 3. The interaction between sex/gender and type of imagined infidelity. While you should be able to come up with three sets of orthogonal contrast coefficients that capture each of these effects, individually, I have not asked you to do so.

  • Given that these effects are confounded in the one-way ANOVA, I recommend that you present the results this way: First, make clear what the groups are and what the outcome variable is. Second, indicate whether or not the groups differed significantly on the outcome variable. Third, present the results of the pairwise comparisons.

  • Be very careful with respect to identification of the comparison/outcome variable.  It is not "pulse rate."

  • Do note that our design is partly experimental, partly non-experimental.  Some would call it "quasi-experimental."  We have manipulated one variable (type of imagined infidelity) but not the other (sex/gender).
